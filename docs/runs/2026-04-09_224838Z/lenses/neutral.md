## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The April 8 ceasefire is **structurally unstable** and operating under fundamentally incompatible interpretations. Fresh data confirms the simulation's core insight: all parties are treating this as a tactical pause rather than genuine de-escalation. The Lebanon scope ambiguity is not a drafting error—it is being actively weaponized by both Iran and Israel to continue operations while claiming compliance.

**Key divergence from simulation**: The fresh data shows *higher* intensity Israeli operations in Lebanon than the simulation predicted, and *lower* immediate Hezbollah retaliation than simulated. This suggests either (a) Hezbollah is exercising more restraint than modeled, or (b) the authorization chain from Tehran is slower/more conditional than the simulation assumed.

**Central forecast**: The ceasefire will not survive the 1-week mark. The most likely break point is **72-96 hours** (April 12-13), triggered by the intersection of three factors: (1) Israeli strikes killing a high-value Hezbollah target in Beirut, (2) Hezbollah retaliation exceeding the 20-30 rocket threshold Iran privately signaled, and (3) IRGC hardliners using the incident to override Khamenei's restraint.

**Why unlikely**: Requires all actors to prioritize long-term stability over short-term tactical advantage. No actor has demonstrated this willingness. Fresh data shows all sides preparing for war resumption, not peace consolidation.

**What it looks like**:

**Fresh data support**: This is essentially what's happening now—Israel striking Lebanon, Hezbollah pausing but not disarming, Iran keeping Hormuz partially open, all sides claiming ceasefire compliance while violating spirit.
