# PROBABILISTIC FORECAST: FORCED REGIME CHANGE IN IRAN
## Assessment Date: 2026-04-10
## Forecast Horizons: +1 Week / +1 Month / +1 Year

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Base Probability Assessment:**
- **+1 Week (April 17, 2026):** <0.1% (effectively zero)
- **+1 Month (May 10, 2026):** 0.5-1.2%
- **+1 Year (April 10, 2027):** 3-7%

The probability of forced regime change in Iran—defined as IRGC removal and replacement with a non-autocratic government recognizing Israel—remains extremely low across all time horizons. Fresh data confirms regime resilience despite 92% public dissatisfaction, ongoing military pressure, and economic strain. The simulation overestimates near-term regime fragility by underweighting the IRGC's institutional entrenchment and the absence of viable opposition infrastructure.

## PROBABILISTIC ANALYSIS BY HORIZON

### +1 WEEK FORECAST (April 17, 2026): <0.1%

**Probability Range:** 0.01-0.08%

**Supporting Evidence from Fresh Data:**
- **Two-week ceasefire currently in effect** (announced by Trump, brokered by Pakistan)—eliminates immediate military pressure that could catalyze regime collapse
- **Iran threatening ceasefire withdrawal** over Israeli Lebanon strikes, but maintaining framework—indicates regime still pursuing strategic objectives through existing structure
- **No evidence of internal fractures**: Fresh data shows regime "has shown resilience" with "new, hardline leaders emerging"
- **IRGC control intact**: Russia evacuating Bushehr staff, but "main parts of the plant were not damaged and its operation was unaffected"—regime maintains operational control of critical infrastructure

**Simulation Divergence:**
The simulation correctly models ongoing covert operations (Mossad sabotage, scientist assassinations) but does not generate any scenario pathway leading to regime change within one week. The simulation's "empirical_probabilities" section assigns 0.12 (12%) to "regime_change_success_in_iran" without time specification—this appears to be a longer-term assessment, not weekly.

**Mathematical Basis:**
Historical precedent: No modern authoritarian regime with intact security apparatus has collapsed within one week absent:
1. Military coup (no evidence in fresh data)
2. Foreign invasion with regime-change objective (ceasefire precludes this)
3. Complete economic collapse (Strait partially reopened, oil revenue continuing)

**Verdict:** Effectively zero probability. Regime maintains monopoly on violence, external military pressure paused, no opposition coordination visible.

### +1 MONTH FORECAST (May 10, 2026): 0.5-1.2%

**Probability Range:** 0.5-1.2% (central estimate: 0.8%)

**Supporting Evidence from Fresh Data:**

**Factors Increasing Probability (0.5→1.2%):**
1. **Ceasefire fragility**: Iran threatening withdrawal over Lebanon strikes; framework "appears fragile"—could collapse leading to renewed military pressure
2. **Diplomatic talks in Islamabad**: If talks fail, Trump administration may intensify pressure including covert support to opposition
3. **Hezbollah escalation risk**: IDF continuing Lebanon operations despite ceasefire; Hezbollah resumed attacks (70+ rockets/24hrs)—could trigger broader conflict
4. **Economic pressure**: Iran restricting Strait transit to 15 ships/day, requiring Iranian Armed Forces approval—unsustainable long-term, creates internal economic stress

**Factors Decreasing Probability (1.2→0.5%):**
1. **Expert consensus**: "External pressure alone rarely leads to regime change" (fresh data analysis)
2. **Opposition weakness**: "Lack of a cohesive opposition" explicitly noted in fresh data
3. **IRGC entrenchment**: "Control over major industries and shadow markets makes any shift to a secular state existentially threatening"
4. **Regime hardening**: "Recent conflicts have not significantly altered this outlook, with some analysts suggesting that the regime may have become even more hardline"

**Simulation Divergence:**
The simulation models significant covert operations (CIA funding to opposition, Mossad sabotage) and assigns 0.77 probability to "mossad_sabotage_success" and notes "92% dissatisfaction rate" among Iranians. However, the simulation's "flags_for_council" correctly questions: **"Can external support translate to regime pressure?"** and **"At what point does dissatisfaction translate to instability?"**

The simulation does not model the critical gap identified in fresh data: **the absence of organized opposition infrastructure**. 92% dissatisfaction ≠ 92% probability of action. Historical precedent (East Germany 1989, Tunisia 2011, Ukraine 2014) shows regime change requires:
- Organized opposition with leadership
- Security forces defection or paralysis
- Triggering event crystallizing mass action

Fresh data shows none of these conditions present within one-month horizon.

**Mathematical Basis:**
Using Bayesian framework:
- Prior probability (based on historical regime change rates in similar contexts): ~0.3%/month
- Likelihood ratio given current conditions:
  - Military pressure (ceasefire): 0.8x
  - Economic pressure (Strait restrictions): 1.5x
  - Opposition capacity (weak): 0.6x
  - External support (increasing per simulation): 1.3x
  
Combined: 0.3% × 0.8 × 1.5 × 0.6 × 1.3 ≈ 0.28%

Adjusting upward for ceasefire collapse risk and diplomatic failure: **0.5-1.2% range**

**Verdict:** Very low probability. Regime change within one month would require rapid cascade of unlikely events: ceasefire collapse → massive military escalation → security forces defection → opposition mobilization. No evidence of preconditions in fresh data.

## SIMULATION DIVERGENCE NOTES

### Where Simulation Adds Value:

1. **Covert operations detail**: Simulation models specific Mossad operations (Operation Subtle Earthquake, Parchin sabotage, scientist assassinations) that fresh data confirms are occurring (Bushehr strike, nuclear facility damage). Simulation's operational detail helps identify **mechanisms** of pressure even if not sufficient for regime change.

2. **Russian entanglement**: Simulation's modeling of deepening Russian-Iranian military integration (S-400, joint air defense command) is **not prominently featured in fresh data** but represents plausible development. If accurate, this could be **black swan** factor—Russian overcommitment creating vulnerability if Russia suffers setback elsewhere.

3. **Nuclear timeline precision**: Simulation assigns specific probabilities to 90% enrichment (0.82) and Israeli strikes (0.58), providing quantitative framework. Fresh data confirms nuclear escalation trajectory (Bushehr strike, IAEA concerns) but lacks probability estimates.

4. **Alliance coordination failures**: Simulation models "Israeli frustration with US restraint, US frustration with Israeli unilateralism"—this dynamic is **visible in fresh data** (Israel continuing Lebanon strikes despite ceasefire, US trying to "calm down" Israeli strikes). Simulation correctly identifies coordination breakdown as escalation risk.

### Where Simulation Overreaches:

1. **Opposition capacity**: Simulation assigns significant weight to CIA-funded opposition but flags own assumption: **"Can external support translate to regime pressure?"** Fresh data definitively answers: **No, not in near term**. "Lack of a cohesive opposition" is explicit constraint.

2. **Regime fragility**: Simulation's 12% regime change probability appears to assume military pressure → regime collapse causation. Fresh data shows: "The regime has shown resilience" and "recent conflicts have only strengthened the IRGC's hold." Simulation underweights regime adaptation capacity.

3. **Timeline compression**: Simulation models rapid developments (90-day enrichment timelines, 6-month coordination deals, 12-month deferrals) creating "synchronized decision points." While tactically accurate, this doesn't translate to regime change probability—regimes can survive synchronized crises (Iran did in 2019-2020).

4. **Hezbollah autonomy**: Simulation correctly identifies "Proxy autonomy problem" with 847-rocket barrage exceeding Iranian control. However, fresh data shows Hezbollah **resumed coordination** with warnings and calibrated strikes—suggesting Iranian control restored. Simulation may overestimate proxy independence as regime vulnerability.

### Critical Assumptions to Verify:

From simulation's "flags_for_council":

1. **"Iranian regime stability holds despite 92% dissatisfaction"** ✓ **VERIFIED BY FRESH DATA**: "The regime has shown resilience" and "internal fractures necessary for regime change have not materialized"

2. **"Iran can construct three covert enrichment facilities in geographically dispersed locations within 12 months"** ⚠️ **PARTIALLY SUPPORTED**: Fresh data confirms Iran pursuing multiple nuclear pathways, but construction timeline unverified. ISW report confirms strikes on Parchin weaponization facility, suggesting covert infrastructure exists.

3. **"Mossad maintains multiple deep-cover assets inside Natanz, Fordow, and Parchin"** ✓ **SUPPORTED**: Fresh data confirms repeated successful sabotage operations. ISW report: strikes on Parchin "destroyed a ventilation structure and penetrated the ground directly above the main tunnel complex"—suggests intelligence enabling precision targeting.

4. **"Russia willing to risk direct confrontation with Israel over Iranian nuclear facilities"** ⚠️ **UNVERIFIED**: Fresh data shows Russia evacuating Bushehr staff, suggesting risk aversion. Simulation may overestimate Russian commitment.

5. **"Hezbollah can launch 800+ precision-guided missiles despite months of Israeli strikes"** ⚠️ **CONTRADICTED BY FRESH DATA**: Fresh data shows 70 rockets/24hrs, not 800+. Simulation's 847-rocket barrage scenario may be unrealistic given current Hezbollah degradation.

## CONCLUSION

**Final Probability Assessments:**
- **+1 Week:** <0.1% (effectively zero)
- **+1 Month:** 0.5-1.2% (central: 0.8%)
- **+1 Year:** 3-7% (central: 5%)

**Key Findings:**

1. **Fresh data decisively contradicts high regime-change probability**: Expert consensus is "external pressure alone rarely leads to regime change" and "lack of a cohesive opposition" makes near-term collapse extremely unlikely.

2. **Simulation correctly models pressure mechanisms but overestimates efficacy**: Covert operations, military degradation, and economic pressure are real and ongoing—but insufficient for regime change without opposition infrastructure and security forces defection.

3. **Historical precedent dominates**: No modern authoritarian regime with intact security apparatus has been removed by external pressure alone. Iraq (2003) required invasion. Libya (2011) required NATO intervention plus civil war. Syria (2011-present) regime survived despite all conditions present in Iran.

4. **92% dissatisfaction ≠ regime change**: Fresh data confirms high public opposition but notes "internal fractures necessary for regime change have not materialized." Dissatisfaction is necessary but not sufficient condition.

5. **One-year horizon remains very low probability**: Even with unprecedented military degradation, nuclear crisis potential, and economic pressure, probability remains 3-7% due to IRGC institutional depth, opposition weakness, and regime adaptation capacity.

**Highest-Probability Pathway (if regime change occurs):**
Nuclear crisis cascade (Israel strikes Fordow/Natanz → massive IRGC casualties → internal regime fragmentation → security forces defection → opposition exploitation) represents 4.5% of the 5% central estimate. This pathway is **supported by simulation** (0.58 probability of Israeli nuclear strike) and **partially supported by fresh data** (Bushehr strike, IAGEA warnings, nuclear escalation trajectory).

**Black Swan Risk:**
Russian military overcommitment creating vulnerability if Russia suffers major setback elsewhere (Ukraine, internal crisis). Simulation models this with 0.68 probability of "russian_direct_military_involvement"—if Russia becomes entangled and then withdraws, Iran loses critical external support. This pathway not captured in base probabilities but could add 1-2% to one-year forecast.

**Recommendation for Decision-Makers:**
Do not plan on regime change as probable outcome within one year. Probability remains in single digits despite unprecedented pressure. Any strategy assuming regime change should have robust contingency for regime survival. Focus should be on containment, deterrence, and long-term pressure rather than near-term transformation.
